Why contradictions happen in your current setup
Problem 1: Different factors operate on different time horizons

Some factors are:

long-term: ROIC, margins, capital allocation

medium-term: revenue growth, FCF growth

short-term: RSI, MA50, 52-week range

A stock can be:

amazing over 5 years

expensive over 12 months

overbought over 2 weeks

If you don’t label the time horizon clearly, it sounds contradictory.

Problem 2: Some factors are correlated, some are opposites

A lot of your 120 factors probably overlap heavily.

For example:

ROIC

ROE

operating margin

FCF margin

gross margin

asset turnover

These may all be shouting some version of “good business.”

Then one valuation factor says “expensive” and suddenly the model feels split.

That’s not balanced evidence — that’s 6 cousins on one side and 1 judge on the other.

So if you’re not careful, you either:

overweight correlated clusters, or

show lots of repeated green checkmarks that don’t add real information

Problem 3: Factor signs may be inconsistent

This is a classic bug.

Some metrics are good when high:

ROIC

FCF yield

revenue growth

Some are good when low:

debt/equity

P/E

EV/EBITDA

accrual ratio in many cases

If any factor direction is wrong, or inconsistently normalized, you get garbage suggestions that look sophisticated.

Problem 4: Missing data creates false neutrality

You already found:

leadership conviction falls back to static lookup

So some companies are getting judged with 120 factors, while others are really getting judged on maybe 80 plus some fake defaults.

That creates contradictory output because users think all stocks are being evaluated on the same basis.

They aren’t.

Problem 5: UI language is too recommendation-like

This may be the biggest product issue.

If a factor card says:

“Negative”

users naturally think each factor is a mini recommendation.

But many factors should just say:

That’s a description, not a vote.

What the real fix is

You need to stop treating 120 factors as 120 opinions.

Treat them as:
raw ingredients → grouped signals → one decision engine

The clean architecture
1. Put every factor into one category only

Every factor should belong to exactly one bucket:

Quality

Growth

Valuation

Financial strength

Momentum

Timing

Risk

Sentiment

No orphan factors. No mystery factors.

2. Tag every factor by role

Each factor should have metadata:

role: descriptive | scoring | gating | explanatory

horizon: short | medium | long

direction: higher_better | lower_better | neutral_range

missing_policy: ignore | impute | penalize

bucket_weight

This is huge.

Because then you can say:

RSI is a short-term timing factor

ROIC is a long-term quality factor

FCF yield is a valuation factor

accrual ratio is a risk/quality factor

Now contradictions become understandable instead of chaotic.

3. Only let some factors drive the verdict

Not every factor should affect the final recommendation.

For example:

70 factors may help create sub-scores

20 may be for display only

10 may act as risk flags

5 may gate the final recommendation

That makes the engine much more stable.

4. Introduce hard conflict rules

A few examples:

No STRONG BUY if valuation is bottom decile and entry timing is poor

No BUY if financial strength is below threshold

No BUY if accounting quality is missing and leverage is high

Cap verdict at ADD when data coverage < 70%

This is how you stop “amazing business, terrible setup, still BUY” weirdness.

How to handle contradictions in the UI

Instead of pretending there’s one truth, show:

Business quality

Strong

Valuation

Stretched

Momentum

Positive

Entry timing

Weak

Overall verdict

ADD — great company, not a great entry today

That is not contradictory. That is useful.

These may help storytelling, but they shouldn’t quietly swing a verdict unless they are rigorously defined.
