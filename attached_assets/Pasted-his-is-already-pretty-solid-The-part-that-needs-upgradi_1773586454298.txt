his is already pretty solid. The part that needs upgrading is not the math first, it’s the decision narrative layer.

Right now the output is strong on scoring, but a human investor still has to mentally answer:

Why does this score matter?

What is the actual bet?

What would make me buy now vs wait?

What could prove me wrong?

What kind of position does this deserve?

That’s where the system becomes much more useful.

What to improve
1. Separate score from decision

A score is not a decision.

A stock with 55/100 is not automatically an “Add.”
It depends on:

current portfolio exposure

alternatives available

whether the thesis is clear

whether the catalyst is visible

whether the downside is acceptable

whether you already own it

So after the score, add a Decision Layer:

Verdict: Avoid / Watch / Starter / Add / Build / Hold / Trim

Why now: one sentence

What must happen next: one sentence

What breaks the thesis: one sentence

That makes it actionable.

2. Add a Thesis Type

Every stock should be classified before scoring narrative is shown.

For example:

Compounder

Quality at Reasonable Price

Turnaround

Expectation Reset

Mispriced Optionality

Cyclical Recovery

Tactical Rebound

Overhyped Quality

Value Trap Risk

This matters because a 55 score in a compounder is very different from a 55 score in a turnaround.

3. Add a 3-part narrative structure

For decision-making, I’d make every stock report answer only 3 things:

A. What is true?

Business reality.

Example:
“Adobe remains a high-quality software franchise with exceptional margins, strong cash generation, and durable customer lock-in.”

B. What is priced in?

Market expectation.

Example:
“The market is no longer pricing Adobe like a premium hyper-growth name; current multiples imply moderated expectations compared with prior years.”

C. What could change?

Catalyst / rerating / failure point.

Example:
“For the stock to work meaningfully from here, Adobe likely needs either renewed growth confidence, margin durability, or evidence that AI/product innovation can support another leg of monetisation.”

That structure is very strong because it mirrors how good investors think.

4. Add a Decision Tension

This is the single most useful improvement.

Every stock should have a line like:

Core tension:
“Excellent business, but only modest discount and no strong near-term rerating trigger.”

That tells you the real issue immediately.

Other examples:

“Cheap stock, but quality is deteriorating.”

“Strong business, but expectations still leave little room for error.”

“Messy current numbers, but evidence suggests temporary rather than structural impairment.”

“Narrative is exciting, but valuation already assumes success.”

This is gold because it compresses the whole case into one sentence.

5. Add a Trigger Framework

The score alone is static. Investing is dynamic.

For each stock, include:

Buy trigger

Upgrade trigger

Trim trigger

Exit trigger

For Adobe, maybe:

Buy trigger: valuation falls further or revisions stabilize

Upgrade trigger: AI monetisation translates into renewed revenue acceleration

Trim trigger: sharp multiple rerating without matching earnings change

Exit trigger: structural slowdown in core franchise or margin erosion without recovery

Now the system becomes a decision engine, not just a rating page.

6. Reduce “No data” clutter

Too many “No data” items weaken confidence in the report.

Better options:

hide factors with missing data from the public-facing page

show a data confidence score

say: “Score confidence: Moderate (6/10 factors available)”

That reads much better than scattering “No data” everywhere.

7. Add confidence in the score

A stock with 55 based on rich data is different from 55 with half the factors missing.

So show:

Score: 55

Confidence: Medium

Coverage: 68% of model inputs present

That makes the output feel more intelligent and honest.

8. Add a Positioning Logic paragraph

Not just “Starter.”

Explain why.

Example:
“Starter size is appropriate because quality is attractive, but the discount is narrow and the mispricing case is not yet strongly confirmed. This looks more like an accumulating-on-weakness name than an immediate high-conviction add.”

That’s much more useful than just the label.

The goal is simple:

Scores tell you what the stock is.
Narrative tells you what to do about it.

Investment Decision Narrative Template
1. One-line verdict

[Ticker] is a [starter / add / hold / avoid / watchlist] because [business quality] is [strong/moderate/weak], while the stock is [undervalued/fairly valued/overvalued] and the main risk is [key fragility].

Example:
ADBE is a starter position because business quality is strong, but the stock opportunity is only moderate and the market is not clearly mispricing it enough yet to justify a full allocation.

2. Business quality narrative

Describe whether the company is fundamentally worth owning.

Template

The business appears [high / medium / low] quality, supported by [ROIC], [margin structure], [cash generation], and [competitive durability].
The main strengths are [top 2–3 strengths].
The main weakness is [main weakness], which matters because [why it matters].

Example for Adobe

The business appears high quality, supported by very high ROIC, elite gross margins, strong operating profitability, and excellent free cash flow generation. These are signs of a durable software franchise with pricing power and recurring economics. The main strengths are capital efficiency, cash conversion, and margin structure. The main weakness is balance-sheet liquidity metrics not looking particularly strong on a current-ratio basis, though for a mature software business this may not be a thesis-breaker.

3. Stock opportunity narrative

This is the “why now?” section.

Template

The stock setup is [strong / moderate / weak]. Valuation looks [cheap / fair / expensive] relative to [history / peers / cash flow]. However, the entry case is weakened by [lack of catalyst / already rerated / weak technicals / full expectations].
At current levels, upside depends on [multiple rerating / earnings upgrades / margin normalization / market sentiment improvement].

Example

The stock setup is moderate. Adobe screens attractively on forward PE, EV/EBITDA, and free cash flow yield, which suggests the stock is not obviously expensive on conventional valuation metrics. However, the entry case is weakened by the lack of a strong margin-of-safety signal and limited evidence of a near-term rerating catalyst. At current levels, upside likely depends more on steady earnings delivery than on a dramatic valuation rerating.

4. Mispricing narrative

This is the heart of the thesis. Why is the market wrong?

Template

The mispricing case is [strong / partial / weak].
The market may be underestimating [normalization / optionality / resilience / earnings power] because [temporary issue].
The evidence supporting this is [cash quality / revisions / improving margins / overlooked segment / cyclical trough].
The evidence against it is [no catalyst / structural risk / limited discount / weak confirmation].

Example

The mispricing case is only partial. The strongest support comes from cash quality, with free cash flow exceeding accounting earnings, which suggests the underlying economics may be better than headline optics imply. However, the case is not yet fully convincing because there is limited evidence of a clear temporary issue that will reverse, and the margin of safety appears very small. This makes Adobe more of a good business at a reasonable price than an obvious market mistake.

5. Expectation risk narrative

This stops you from buying great companies at dumb prices.

Template

Expectation risk is [low / moderate / high].
The market is currently pricing in [low / moderate / aggressive] execution assumptions, based on [valuation multiples / reverse DCF / margins / peer premium].
That means the stock is vulnerable if [growth slows / margins compress / sentiment resets / AI hype fades].

Example

Expectation risk is moderate. Traditional valuation multiples do not suggest extreme optimism, but Adobe’s already-high margins mean the business has less room for operational upside than lower-margin companies undergoing improvement. This means the stock is more vulnerable to disappointment from growth deceleration than from valuation excess alone.

6. Fragility narrative

This section answers: what breaks the thesis?

Template

Fragility appears [low / moderate / high].
The thesis is most exposed to [competitive pressure / regulation / leverage / cyclicality / execution / concentration].
The most important thing to monitor is [single variable], because deterioration there would likely invalidate the thesis.

Example

Fragility appears moderate to low. Adobe’s core business model is resilient, but the thesis remains exposed to competitive pressure, innovation risk, and the possibility that AI-related product shifts weaken pricing power over time. The most important thing to monitor is whether revenue growth and monetization remain durable without sacrificing margins.

7. Decision rule

This turns narrative into action.

Template

Decision: [Starter / Add / Hold / Avoid / Watchlist]

Why:

Own it because [top reason]

Do not size bigger yet because [main limiting factor]

Upgrade to Add if [specific trigger]

Downgrade to Avoid/Hold if [failure condition]

Example

Decision: Starter

Why:

Own it because the underlying business quality is excellent.

Do not size bigger yet because the mispricing case is not strong enough and the discount to intrinsic value looks thin.

Upgrade to Add if earnings revisions improve, valuation becomes more dislocated, or a clearer catalyst emerges.

Downgrade to Hold/Avoid if growth decelerates further without a compensating valuation reset.**

Example using your Adobe case
Adobe Narrative Example

Verdict
Adobe is a starter because it is clearly a high-quality business, but the stock does not yet offer a strong enough mispricing or catalyst setup to justify aggressive buying.

Business quality
Adobe remains a high-quality compounder. Its extremely high gross margins, operating margins, and free cash flow margins suggest a durable software moat with strong pricing power and high incremental economics. ROIC is exceptional, which supports the view that the company still creates substantial economic value. Cash generation appears real rather than accounting-driven, which strengthens confidence in the franchise.

Stock opportunity
The stock looks reasonably valued on forward earnings, EV/EBITDA, and free cash flow yield. That makes it investable, but not obviously compelling. The weak point is that the margin of safety is very small, so the valuation case is more “acceptable” than “attractive.” In other words, the stock is not expensive enough to avoid, but not cheap enough to press hard.

Mispricing
The market may be somewhat underappreciating Adobe’s cash quality and durability, but the evidence of true mispricing is incomplete. There is no strong temporary issue that is clearly depressing earnings power, and there is not yet a visible rerating catalyst. So this is not a classic neglected or misunderstood opportunity. It is more a case of a good business trading near fair value.

Expectation risk
Expectation risk is not extreme on headline multiples, but it still exists because Adobe is already a mature, highly profitable business. When margins are already elite, upside from further operating improvement is harder to find. That means future returns depend more heavily on sustaining growth and defending the moat.

Fragility
The thesis is relatively resilient, but not bulletproof. Competitive disruption, especially around AI-enabled creative tools and workflow shifts, is the key long-term variable. If Adobe loses pricing power or user engagement, the premium quality case weakens quickly.

Decision
Start with a small position only. Adobe deserves a place on the watchlist or in a starter allocation because of its business quality, but it does not yet qualify as a high-conviction dislocation. Increase only if the stock gets materially cheaper or if evidence of renewed earnings acceleration emerges.
