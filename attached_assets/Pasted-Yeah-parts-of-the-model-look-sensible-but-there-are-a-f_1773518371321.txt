What looks weird

Here’s where I’d get suspicious.

1. Some ROIC numbers are absurd

Example:

ABBV ROIC = 6225.0%

RR.L ROIC = 623.5%

RMV.L ROIC = 266.1%

Sometimes very high ROIC can happen because of accounting quirks, negative/very small invested capital, buybacks, goodwill distortions, or denominator problems.

Like this:

If I say “I made £10 using only £1,” that’s 1000%.
But if the “£1” is weird accounting math and not real capital, the number is kind of fake-pretty.

So if your model treats these values as normal, it can get tricked.

Fix: cap or normalize ROIC.
For example:

anything above 100% gets clipped

or use percentile ranking instead of raw values

or use 3-year median ROIC

2. FCF yield is dominating some names in odd ways

Example:

TSM FCF yield 36.7% jumps off the page as unusually high

Tencent 20.5% also very high

some mega caps have tiny yields, which may be fine, but the spread looks wide enough that I’d verify the formula

Sometimes people accidentally use:

FCF / revenue
instead of

FCF / enterprise value or market cap

or mix TTM and forward values.

So I’d check:
How exactly are you calculating FCF yield?

Because if that input is wrong, a lot of verdicts will look “smart” while actually being built on a broken ruler.

3. The verdict mapping is not fully consistent

A few examples:

PLTR gets 0.82 / 0.80 / 0.37, rev growth +70%, but only ADD

What I would test next
1. Check the formulas

Especially:

ROIC

FCF Yield

Revenue Growth basis

whether values are trailing, forward, annualized, or mixed

2. Winsorize extreme values

That’s a fancy way of saying:
don’t let crazy numbers bully the model

For example:

ROIC cap at 100%

revenue growth cap at maybe 80% or 100%

FCF yield cap at maybe 15–20%

3. Turn raw values into percentile scores

Instead of saying:

6225% ROIC is way better than 60%

say:

both are just “top bucket”

This prevents nonsense explosions.

4. Make the verdict formula explicit

For example:

STRONG BUY = Fortress > 0.8 and Rocket > 0.75 and Wave > 0.65 and no red flags

BUY = two strong factors plus acceptable valuation

ADD = good company, less attractive setup

HOLD = quality okay, upside weaker

TRIM = great company, stretched setup or deteriorating trend

If you can’t explain the label rule in one breath, it’s probably too muddy.

5. Separate quality from valuation

Right now it feels like you might be mixing:

business quality

growth

cash generation

maybe valuation

That’s okay, but then the verdict should reflect that clearly.

A good company is not always a good stock today.

For example:

Ferrari can be an amazing business and still be a TRIM

Merck can be slower growth but attractive if valuation/cash flow is strong

That distinction matters a lot.
