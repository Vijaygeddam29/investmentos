ou’re basically saying two things:

The obvious great companies are usually expensive, so future returns get pulled forward.

The cheap companies may be cheap for a reason, so “undervalued” is often a trap.

That is exactly the tension every serious stock picker runs into. You do not really “solve” it once and for all. You solve it by building a decision process that is robust when your classification is partly wrong.

The core fix

Stop treating valuation and quality as a single question.

They are two separate questions:

How good is the business?

What future is already priced in?

A fortress company can still be a bad investment if expectations are absurd.
A mediocre company can still be a good investment if expectations are too pessimistic.

So the answer is not “buy only undervalued” or “buy only great businesses.”
It is: buy where the gap between implied expectations and likely reality is most favorable.

A cleaner way to think about your model

Your buckets like fortress, growth, wave are useful, but they need a second axis.

Instead of only classifying the business, classify the market expectation state too.

For example:

Fortress + euphoric pricing

Fortress + fair pricing

Fortress + temporary dislocation

Growth + underappreciated optionality

Growth + perfect execution priced in

Wave + momentum only

Wave + cyclical trough

Broken/cheap + value trap

That helps because “overpriced” is not a business label. It is an expectations label.

The practical way out of the dilemma

You need 4 layers.

1. Separate business quality from stock attractiveness

Score the business on things like:

returns on capital

gross margin stability

reinvestment runway

customer lock-in

pricing power

balance sheet strength

management capital allocation

Then separately score the stock on:

forward EV/EBIT

forward P/E

free cash flow yield

PEG

implied margin assumptions

implied growth assumptions

valuation versus its own history

valuation versus peers

A top business with a terrible stock setup should not get a big weight.

2. Use scenario pricing, not point estimates

A lot of people get trapped because they say:
“Fair value is 140 and stock is 110, therefore undervalued.”

That’s too neat for reality.

Instead build 3 cases:

bear

base

bull

And assign probabilities.

Then ask:

what revenue growth is needed to justify today’s price?

what margins are needed?

what reinvestment burden is needed?

how fragile are those assumptions?

This is where the truth usually pops out.

A stock may look expensive on near-term P/E but cheap if the market is underestimating 5-year operating leverage.
Or it may look cheap on P/E while the whole valuation depends on peak-cycle margins that won’t last.

3. Require a reason for mispricing

Never buy just because something screens cheap.

Ask: why is the market wrong, specifically?

Good answers:

temporary cyclical weakness

one-off regulatory fear

misunderstood product transition

accounting noise

capex depressing current earnings but strengthening moat

short-term margin compression with intact long-term economics

Bad answers:

“it used to trade higher”

“the P/E is low”

“it’s a good company”

“people are too negative”

If you cannot explain the source of mispricing, you probably do not have an edge.

4. Size positions by confidence in both quality and valuation

This is the big one.

Your portfolio should not be equally weighted just because names passed a screen.

Think like this:

High quality + fair/slightly cheap + strong balance sheet
Bigger position

High quality + expensive + still compounding
Smaller starter position or watchlist

Cheap but uncertain
Small, speculative weight only

Wave / cyclical / narrative stock
Tactical sizing, not core capital

That way you don’t need to be perfect. Your process carries the load.

What usually works best in practice

The best opportunities often sit in one of these zones:

Fortress businesses with temporary disappointment

These are usually the cleanest setups.

Examples in principle:

short-term growth scare

one bad quarter

temporary margin pressure

product cycle pause

The business remains strong, but the stock de-rates enough to create return potential.

Growth companies where the market underestimates duration

This is harder but powerful.

The market often gets near-term growth right and long-term runway wrong.

That is where multi-baggers come from:
not from “cheap stocks,” but from underestimated duration of compounding.

Cyclicals at trough when balance sheets survive

This is a different game.
You’re betting on normalization, not long-term moat compounding.
That can work, but it should be a different sleeve in the portfolio.

What to avoid

Three common traps:

1. Paying any price for quality

Great company, bad return.
This is how people underperform while feeling smart.

2. Confusing statistical cheapness with undervaluation

A low multiple is often just the market pricing in deterioration correctly.

3. Acting like precision is possible

Valuation is a range, not a single true number.
Your edge comes from being approximately right with discipline, not exactly right with spreadsheets.

A better decision rule

You can make your model stronger with a simple rule:

Only buy when at least two of the following three are true:

business quality is high

valuation is attractive relative to realistic scenarios

there is a clear identifiable catalyst or mispricing reason

That helps filter out both glamour traps and fake cheapness.

The philosophical answer

The dilemma exists because stocks are not priced on “quality” alone.
They’re priced on quality relative to expectation.

So the real question is never:
“Is this a good company?”

It is:
“Is reality likely to be better, worse, or roughly equal to what this price already assumes?”

That shift fixes a lot.

Absolutely. Here’s a clean way to turn those four into something you can actually score.

The key is: each category should use observable data points, not vibes. Then you convert those into a score and finally into a position size.

1. Expectation score

This measures how much success is already priced in.

A high expectation score means the market is already assuming a lot of good news. That usually lowers future return potential unless the company keeps beating those assumptions.

Data points to use
Valuation versus own history

Current forward P/E vs 5-year average forward P/E

Current EV/EBIT vs 5-year average

Current EV/Sales vs 5-year average

Current FCF yield vs 5-year average

Interpretation:

If valuation is far above history, expectations are high

If near or below history, expectations are more moderate

Valuation versus peers

Forward P/E relative to sector median

EV/EBITDA relative to peer median

PEG ratio relative to peers

Price/sales relative to peer group for similar margin profile

Interpretation:

Premium only makes sense if growth, margins, and moat clearly justify it

Embedded growth expectations

You want to infer what the market is assuming.

Useful proxies:

Analyst consensus 2-year and 3-year revenue growth

Analyst consensus EPS CAGR next 3 years

Current multiple divided by expected growth

Reverse DCF implied revenue growth

Reverse DCF implied terminal margin

Reverse DCF implied reinvestment efficiency

Interpretation:

If price only works under near-perfect growth and margins, expectations are stretched

Price behavior / sentiment

12-month price return vs EPS revision trend

Multiple expansion versus earnings growth contribution

Share of return driven by rerating rather than earnings

Crowding indicators: ownership concentration, hedge fund popularity, passive index weight

Interpretation:

If most gains came from multiple expansion, expectations may be running ahead of fundamentals

Example scoring bands

Score from 1 to 5:

1 = depressed expectations
Below historical multiples, weak sentiment, low implied growth

2 = modest expectations
Fairly priced, no euphoria

3 = normal expectations
Reasonable premium for quality

4 = elevated expectations
Strong premium, high implied execution

5 = euphoric expectations
Market pricing near-perfect outcomes

2. Mispricing reason

This is the most important one because it asks: why is the market wrong?

You should not buy something just because it looks cheap or expensive. You need a reason the current pricing is off.

Data points to use

This category is partly quantitative, partly judgment-based, but it must be evidence-backed.

Temporary dislocation indicators

One-off margin compression

Temporary inventory correction

Product cycle gap

Regulatory overhang with likely resolution

Capex spike depressing near-term earnings

FX headwinds masking local-currency growth

Short-term demand softness versus long-term customer retention stability

Market misunderstanding indicators

Revenue quality improving but not recognized

Mix shift toward higher-margin segments

New business line optionality underappreciated

Hidden pricing power

Cash conversion understated due to accounting treatment

Sum-of-the-parts gap

Stronger balance sheet than headline metrics suggest

Evidence for re-rating

EPS revisions stabilizing or turning positive

Insider buying

Buybacks at attractive valuations

Industry conditions normalizing

Management guidance conservative relative to likely outcomes

New products entering monetization phase

Margin recovery already visible in unit economics

How to score it

This is not “how cheap is it.”
This is “how strong is the reason that price is wrong.”

Score from 0 to 5:

0 = no identified mispricing
Market probably sees the situation correctly

1 = weak or vague thesis
“Feels cheap”

2 = plausible but unproven
Some evidence, still speculative

3 = reasonable and evidence-backed
Clear temporary issue or underappreciated driver

4 = strong
Multiple data points support market misread

5 = exceptional
Very clear disconnect between price and reality

Good mispricing reasons

Market using trough sentiment on a non-impaired moat

Temporary capex obscures long-run FCF

Product transition creates optical weakness

Cyclical trough misread as structural decline

Bad mispricing reasons

Used to trade higher

Cheap on one ratio

Great company, so must recover

Everyone is too negative

3. Fragility score

This measures how easily the thesis can break.

A fragile stock may still work, but it should not be sized like a fortress.

Data points to use
Business fragility

Revenue concentration by product/customer/geography

Gross margin volatility

Operating margin cyclicality

Dependence on one product cycle

Dependence on macro or commodity price

Exposure to regulation or reimbursement

Switching costs actually low despite “moat” story

Technological obsolescence risk

Financial fragility

Net debt / EBITDA

Interest coverage

Current ratio / liquidity runway

Share dilution history

Pension deficits or off-balance-sheet liabilities

Dependence on refinancing markets

Forecast fragility

Wide dispersion in analyst estimates

Frequent guidance misses

High variance in quarterly results

Long duration valuation with little near-term cash support

Very high terminal value share in DCF

Market fragility

High beta

High short interest

Illiquidity

Crowded ownership

Event risk concentration

Stock highly sensitive to rates or one narrative

Scoring bands

Score from 1 to 5:

1 = very resilient
Diverse revenue, strong balance sheet, stable margins

2 = low fragility
Some normal business risk, but manageable

3 = moderate
A few pressure points

4 = fragile
Thesis depends on several things going right

5 = very fragile
One miss can break the story badly

Important:
A stock can be high quality and still have high fragility if valuation is stretched and all future returns depend on flawless execution.

4. Position size rule

This converts all that into portfolio action.

Position sizing is where your model becomes real.

You do not want your biggest positions to be the most exciting names. You want them to be the names where quality is high, expectations are reasonable, mispricing is clear, and fragility is low.

Inputs to use

You should combine:

Business quality score

Expectation score

Mispricing score

Fragility score

Liquidity / conviction / correlation to existing holdings

A simple formula idea:

Position Attractiveness = Quality + Mispricing - Expectation - Fragility

You can modify weighting later.

Example position size bands
Core position: 6% to 10%

Use when:

Quality very high

Mispricing strong

Expectations moderate or low

Fragility low

These are your “fortress at a reasonable or temporarily discounted price” names.

Standard position: 3% to 5%

Use when:

Quality solid

Mispricing reasonable

Some fragility or moderate expectations

These are good ideas, but not slam dunks.

Starter position: 1% to 2.5%

Use when:

High-quality but expensive

Or high-upside but more fragile

Or thesis still developing

This lets you track closely without overcommitting.

Tactical/speculative: 0.5% to 1%

Use when:

Wave / cyclical / event-driven

Cheap but thesis is uncertain

Fragility high

Zero / watchlist

Use when:

No clear mispricing

Expectations already euphoric

Fragility too high for payoff

Thesis depends on macro guesswork more than company edge

A very simple scoring template

Out of 5 each:

Quality: 1–5

Expectation: 1–5

Mispricing: 0–5

Fragility: 1–5

Then:

Net Score = (2 × Quality) + (2 × Mispricing) - Expectation - Fragility

Example ranges:

8+ = core

5 to 7 = standard

2 to 4 = starter

0 to 1 = tactical only

below 0 = avoid/watchlist

That weighting works because quality and mispricing matter more than the others, but you can tune it.

The big principle

You are not trying to find “cheap stocks.”
You are trying to find:

businesses strong enough to survive,

prices low enough to allow returns,

a reason the market is wrong,

and a thesis robust enough not to break easily.

That’s the combo.
